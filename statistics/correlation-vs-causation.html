Correlation vs Causation

Just because two things move together does not mean one causes the other

The One-Liner

Correlation means two variables move together (when one goes up, the other tends to go up or down). Causation means one variable directly causes the other to change.

The Ice Cream and Crime Example

Here is a real statistical finding: ice cream sales and crime rates are positively correlated. When ice cream sales go up, crime goes up too.

Does this mean ice cream causes crime? Should we ban ice cream to reduce crime? Obviously not.

The hidden factor is temperature. When it is hot outside, people buy more ice cream AND more people are outdoors, leading to more opportunities for crime. Temperature is the confounding variable - it drives both ice cream sales and crime rates independently.

Another Way to See It

Drowning deaths and Nicolas Cage movie releases are correlated (this is a real dataset). Nobody believes Nicolas Cage movies cause drowning. The correlation is pure coincidence - or driven by an unrelated third factor (both happen more in summer months, or it is simply a statistical fluke with a small dataset).

Why This Matters in Data Work

  • Business decisions: if you see that users who use Feature X have higher retention, does Feature X cause retention? Or do already-engaged users simply use more features?
  • A/B testing exists for this reason: randomized experiments are the gold standard for establishing causation because they control for confounding variables
  • Dashboard metrics: just because two KPIs trend together does not mean changing one will affect the other

How to Establish Causation

  1. Randomized controlled experiment (A/B test) - randomly assign users to groups and change only one variable
  2. Control for confounders - use regression or matching to isolate the effect of one variable
  3. Look for a mechanism - is there a logical reason why A would cause B?
  4. Check temporal order - A must come before B in time for A to cause B
  5. Replicate - does the relationship hold across different datasets and contexts?

Key Takeaway

Correlation tells you two things are related. It does NOT tell you one causes the other. Always ask: "Is there a confounding variable?" and "Could this be coincidence?" The only reliable way to prove causation is a controlled experiment. When you cannot experiment, be transparent that you are observing a correlation, not proving a cause.
