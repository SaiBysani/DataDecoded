P-Value

The most misunderstood number in data science

Explain It Like I Am 10

Imagine you have a coin and you want to know if it is fair. You flip it 100 times and get 60 heads. Is the coin rigged, or did you just get lucky?

The p-value answers this question: "If the coin really is fair, how likely is it that I would see a result this extreme (or more extreme) just by chance?"

If the p-value is very small (say 0.02), it means there is only a 2% chance of getting 60+ heads with a fair coin. That is pretty unlikely, so you might conclude the coin is not fair.

If the p-value is large (say 0.35), it means results like yours happen 35% of the time with a fair coin. Nothing unusual - probably just normal luck.

The Courtroom Analogy

Think of a courtroom. The defendant (your hypothesis) is "innocent until proven guilty." The p-value is like the strength of the evidence against them. A tiny p-value is overwhelming evidence - enough to convict. A large p-value means the evidence is weak - not enough to reject the claim of innocence.

The Formal Definition

The p-value is the probability of observing a result as extreme as (or more extreme than) the one you got, assuming the null hypothesis is true.

  • Null hypothesis (H0): the default assumption (e.g., "the coin is fair," "there is no difference between groups")
  • Small p-value (< 0.05): the data is unlikely under H0, so we reject it
  • Large p-value (≥ 0.05): the data is consistent with H0, so we fail to reject it

Real Example: A/B Testing

Your company tests two website buttons: blue (control) vs green (variant). After a week:

  • Blue button: 5.0% conversion rate (200 out of 4,000)
  • Green button: 5.8% conversion rate (232 out of 4,000)

The green button looks better, but is this a real difference or just random noise? You run a hypothesis test and get p = 0.11.

Since 0.11 > 0.05, you cannot confidently say the green button is better. The observed difference could easily be due to chance. You need more data or a bigger effect to be sure.

Common Misconception

Wrong: "A p-value of 0.03 means there is a 3% chance the null hypothesis is true."

Right: "A p-value of 0.03 means that if the null hypothesis IS true, there is a 3% chance of seeing data this extreme."

The p-value is about the data, not about the hypothesis. It does not tell you the probability that your hypothesis is correct - it tells you how surprising your data would be if the null hypothesis were true.

The 0.05 Threshold

The commonly used cutoff of 0.05 is a convention, not a law of nature. It means "we accept a 5% risk of wrongly rejecting a true null hypothesis." Different fields use different thresholds - particle physics uses 0.0000003 (5 sigma). The right threshold depends on the consequences of being wrong.

Key Takeaway

A p-value measures how surprising your data is under the assumption that nothing interesting is happening. Small p-value = surprising data = evidence against the status quo. It does NOT tell you the probability your hypothesis is true, and 0.05 is a convention, not a magic number.
